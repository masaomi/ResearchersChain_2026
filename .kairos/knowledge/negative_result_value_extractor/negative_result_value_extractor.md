# Negative Result Value Extractor

## Purpose
Identify and articulate the scientific value of negative, null, or unexpected results.

## Why Negative Results Matter
- Reduce publication bias and prevent wasted effort by others
- Refine theories and narrow hypothesis space
- Establish boundary conditions for phenomena
- Challenge assumptions in the field

## Value Extraction Framework

| Result Type | Value | How to Frame |
|------------|-------|-------------|
| True null (adequate power) | Narrows hypothesis space | "With 80% power to detect effect d=0.5, we found no evidence for..." |
| Unexpected direction | Challenges existing models | "Contrary to prediction, we observed... suggesting the model needs revision" |
| Method doesn't work | Saves others' time | "We found method X unsuitable for Y because..." |
| Replication failure | Questions prior findings | "We could not replicate Z despite following the original protocol" |

## Reporting Negative Results
1. Confirm adequate statistical power (was the study big enough to detect the effect?)
2. Report effect sizes and confidence intervals (not just "p > 0.05")
3. Distinguish "no evidence of effect" from "evidence of no effect"
4. Discuss what this means for the field
5. Consider journals that welcome negative results (PLOS ONE, Journal of Negative Results)

## Common Mistake
"We found no significant difference" ≠ "There is no difference"
Always report: power, effect size, CI, and what the study could and could not detect.
